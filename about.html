Our Story

The idea started with a Cold War–era ammo can for 7.62×51 a friend’s dad gave me. I was using it for baseball cards, but one day I thought: why not use it for ammo? That sent me online, digging through Etsy and eBay for a proper military-style wooden ammo box. What I found was either beat-up surplus or presentation-style boxes that weren’t my style. So I built my own.

I’ve always loved building with my hands — blanket chests, bookshelves, desks — I’ve always had a thing for boxes. Designing ammo storage crates felt natural. My first one copied the Cold War can’s proportions but I tweaked it to hold 20 boxes of 9mm. That became the Freedom Crate Classic.

The hardest part was the handles. My first version used half-inch hemp rope tied through ¾″ holes in the sides — tough, but the inside knots stole capacity and broke the interior seal. I wanted the interior as airtight as possible and full edge-to-edge capacity, so no more holes through the walls. After countless hours, I built a fail-proof, outside-mounted handle system that’s now my proprietary design — rugged, comfortable, and doesn’t compromise the box.

When I showed a Marine buddy, he asked for one to hold his medals. That’s when I realized other people wanted these crates too — not just ammo storage, but functional pieces of art. And I wanted them to be affordable. Even as material costs climbed, I kept pricing in line with cheaper boxes I saw online — while using better wood, real hinges, and stronger hardware. I’m not chasing giant margins; I’m building value.

I kept thinking about that old saying: keep a thousand rounds for each caliber you own. That idea shaped the lineup — from the Classic to the 1000-round .308 storage crate — and the Freedom Crate Footlocker for bug-out and hurricane-prep gear.

Since designing my first box, I haven’t stopped improving them. I’m constantly in R&D — testing corrosion resistance, waterproofing, and air-sealing. I’m currently experimenting with advanced elastomers and silicone-based polymers — ultra-durable, specialized coatings drawn from the same industries that protect heavy equipment and infrastructure worldwide. I’m currently researching how corner brackets and structural braces can reinforce joint strength and improve long-term rigidity. Every new build pushes me to make the crates stronger, sturdier, and more rugged. My goal is for these boxes to last a lifetime — even multiple lifetimes — to be passed down like family heirlooms. That’s why I sign the bottom of each box, and why I design them to be repairable. If something breaks, it shouldn’t mean the whole crate is trash. These boxes are built to endure, be fixed, and keep serving for generations.

What started as a search for a decent ammo box turned into building my own, then inventing a handle system, then building one for a Marine friend, and eventually a whole lineup of purpose-built wooden crates. I never set out to build a company; I just wanted boxes that made sense. Now I build them for people who want the same: storage that’s strong, reliable, and built to last.

The Legacy Project: Where We’re Headed

Our long-term goal is bigger than just building crates—we want to give back. The Legacy Project will be the non-profit side of Freedom Crate Co., created to honor newly retired veterans by gifting them handcrafted crates to hold their medals, awards, and keepsakes.

The project will be funded solely by a percentage of profits from Freedom Crate Co. sales and by donations. With the support of even a few generous donors, we could be building crates for newly retired veterans for years to come.

Veterans will be able to request a crate by verifying their service and choosing their branch, rank, and even a personalized quote under the lid. Once we reach the point where every newly retired veteran can receive a Freedom Crate at no cost, our vision will be complete.

  • Give-Back: provide free handcrafted crates to newly retired veterans.
  • Personalization: branch insignia, rank, initials, or a custom quote under the lid.
  • Funding: supported by Freedom Crate Co. profits and generous donor contributions.
